# Water Scarcity: Is Water Running Out?

*Compiled: March 15, 2026 | Supplemented with live web research*

---

## The Short Answer

Water is **not running out globally** — it's a cycle. But **accessible freshwater is functionally finite** and under extreme stress. The UN declared in January 2026 that the world has entered an **"era of global water bankruptcy"** — meaning we are consuming and degrading freshwater faster than it replenishes.

## 1. Earth's Water Budget

| Category | Volume | % of Total |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Total water on Earth | ~1.386 billion km³ | 100% |
| Saltwater (oceans) | ~1.335 billion km³ | 96.5% |
| All freshwater | ~35 million km³ | 2.5% |
| Locked in glaciers/ice | ~24 million km³ | ~69% of freshwater |
| Groundwater | ~10.6 million km³ | ~30% of freshwater |
| **Accessible surface freshwater** | **~93,100 km³** | **0.007% of total** |

**We live on 0.007% of Earth's water.** The rest is ocean, ice, or deep underground.

## 2. The Water Cycle — Why Water "Doesn't Run Out" But Also Does

### The Hydrologic Cycle
Water constantly cycles: evaporation → clouds → precipitation → rivers/groundwater → evaporation. In theory, it's renewable. **No water is ever "destroyed."**

### But Here's the Problem
- **Rate of replenishment ≠ rate of use.** We're pumping groundwater aquifers faster than rain refills them. The Ogallala Aquifer (US Great Plains) has dropped 30+ meters in places and would take 6,000 years to refill naturally.
- **Location mismatch.** Water evaporated in Arizona doesn't rain back down in Arizona. It might rain into the ocean, or over a different continent. **80% of water used in data center evaporative cooling is lost to the local watershed** ([Source](https://www.eesi.org/articles/view/data-centers-and-water-consumption)).
- **Quality degradation.** Water can be too polluted to use. Climate change is making droughts longer and floods more intense, reducing *usable* water.
- **Glaciers are melting.** 30%+ of glacier mass gone since 1970 in some regions. This is like spending your savings account — the rivers fed by glaciers will eventually run dry.

### Key Concept: Local vs Global
**Water scarcity is always local.** The planet has the same amount of water it always had. But the freshwater available *where people live* is declining. A data center in Sweden using water has zero impact on water scarcity in Sub-Saharan Africa. A data center in Arizona competing with farms and homes for Colorado River water is a very different story.

## 3. The Current Crisis — By the Numbers (2025-2026)

### UN "Water Bankruptcy" Declaration (January 2026)
The UN declared the world has entered an **"era of global water bankruptcy"** — marked by:
- Irreversible losses of natural water capital
- Inability to bounce back to historic baselines
- Systemic overshoot of sustainable water withdrawal rates

Source: [United Nations University](https://unu.edu/inweh/news/world-enters-era-of-global-water-bankruptcy)

### People Affected
| Statistic | Number | Source |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| People lacking safe drinking water | **2.1 billion** (1 in 4 globally) | WHO/UNICEF, Aug 2025 |
| People facing severe water scarcity ≥1 month/year | **4 billion** | UN-Water |
| People lacking safely managed sanitation | **3.4 billion** | WHO/UNICEF |
| People drinking untreated surface water | **106 million** | WHO/UNICEF |
| People who could be displaced by water scarcity by 2030 | **700 million** | UN |

Source: [WHO/UNICEF Report](https://www.who.int/news/item/26-08-2025-1-in-4-people-globally-still-lack-access-to-safe-drinking-water---who--unicef)

### Water Loss
- World loses **324 billion m³ of freshwater per year** — enough for 280 million people ([World Bank, Nov 2025](https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2025/11/04/world-annual-fresh-water-losses-could-supply-280-million-people))
- **50%+ of world's large lakes** have shrunk since early 1990s
- **410 million hectares of wetlands destroyed** (area ≈ the EU)
- Global water demand will **exceed supply by 40% by 2030** (World Economic Forum)

## 5. Framing for the Tool

### The Honest Story (Not FUD, Not Dismissal)

The tool should tell **three truths simultaneously:**

**Truth 1: AI does use water, and it's growing fast.**
- Data center water is up 88% in 5 years (Google)
- AI is the primary driver of that growth
- Some facilities compete with communities for scarce local water
- This is a legitimate concern, not FUD

**Truth 2: AI water is tiny compared to your other water impacts.**
- A year of heavy GPT-4o use (~200 queries/day) ≈ 122 liters — less water than a single hamburger
- Your food choices dwarf your digital choices by 1,000x+
- A single shower uses more water than a week of AI conversations
- Skipping one hamburger saves ~20x more water than a full year of GPT-4o use

**Truth 3: The water crisis is real, but it's about location, access, and agriculture — not AI.**
- 2.1 billion people lack safe water — this is about infrastructure, poverty, and agricultural overuse
- Water scarcity is always local — your AI use in Canada doesn't affect water in Sub-Saharan Africa
- The real leverage points: food system reform, agricultural efficiency, infrastructure investment
- AI could actually *help* solve water problems (crop optimization, leak detection, climate modeling)

### The Empowering Message

> "Your AI usage is real and measurable. Here's exactly how much water it uses. Here's how it compares to everything else in your life. Understanding your full water footprint helps you make informed choices about where to reduce."

This is more powerful than either "AI is destroying the planet" (FUD) or "don't worry about it" (dismissal). It's **honest, contextualized, and empowering.**

### Special Consideration: Vegan/Low-Impact Lifestyles

For people who are already vegan, don't drive, and minimize waste — their overall water footprint is *already dramatically lower* than average. A vegan diet uses roughly 2,000-3,000 L/day in virtual water vs 5,000-7,000 L/day for omnivore. No car eliminates ~50-100 L/day in fuel water. These people are already doing more than most.

For this audience, AI water anxiety is particularly misplaced — they've already eliminated the biggest water consumers from their lives. Their AI usage is an even tinier fraction of an already-small footprint. Showing them that data is liberating, not dismissive.

## 6. The "Data Center Water Doesn't Actually Disappear" Nuance

An important point for your wife's concern specifically:

### What Happens to Data Center Water
1. **~80% evaporates** into the atmosphere from cooling towers
2. **~20% is discharged** to municipal wastewater
3. The evaporated water **re-enters the water cycle** — it becomes clouds and rains somewhere
4. It is **NOT destroyed, contaminated, or removed from Earth**

### But the Local Impact Is Real
- Evaporated water **doesn't rain back in the same place** — it may rain over the ocean or a different region
- Local aquifers and rivers are depleted **right now** even if the water eventually returns somewhere
- In water-stressed areas (Arizona, Texas), this local depletion genuinely competes with human needs
- "Water positive" pledges from tech companies attempt to address this by replenishing local watersheds

### The Net Assessment
- **Global impact:** Negligible. AI water is a rounding error on global freshwater use.
- **Local impact:** Can be significant in water-stressed regions with large data center clusters.
- **Trend:** Growing fast, needs monitoring, but not an existential threat to water supply.

## 7. Environmental Justice: Where Data Centers Get Built

While AI water use is negligible at the global level, the **local** impact depends entirely on *where* facilities are sited — and the evidence shows a pattern of data centers being built in communities with less political power to resist them.

### 7.1 The Siting Pattern

A WRI (World Resources Institute) national analysis of ~700 US data centers found:
- **Nearly half** are in census tracts with above-median environmental burdens (air pollution, water pollution, lack of park access)
- **Two-thirds of data centers built since 2022** are in water-stressed regions (southern Arizona, Colorado River Basin, Texas)
- Many are in areas with higher social vulnerability indicators, including poverty and lower education levels

Source: [WRI — "From Energy Use to Air Quality, the Many Ways Data Centers Affect US Communities"](https://www.wri.org/insights/us-data-center-growth-impacts)

### 7.2 Specific Cases

| Location | What happened | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Bessemer, Alabama** (majority Black city) | Proposed $14.5 billion hyperscale data center requiring 2 million gallons of water per day — paused after community opposition | [UAB Human Rights Blog](https://sites.uab.edu/humanrights/2025/10/02/construction-and-consequences-the-human-impacts-of-artificial-intelligence-data-centers/) |
| **Mansfield, Georgia** | Residents reported data centers draining wells and aquifers, leaving locals without functional running water | [UAB Human Rights Blog](https://sites.uab.edu/humanrights/2025/10/02/construction-and-consequences-the-human-impacts-of-artificial-intelligence-data-centers/) |
| **Richland Parish, Louisiana** | Meta facility "nearly as big as Manhattan" in rural area | [UAB Human Rights Blog](https://sites.uab.edu/humanrights/2025/10/02/construction-and-consequences-the-human-impacts-of-artificial-intelligence-data-centers/) |
| **San Marcos, Texas** | City council paused $1.5 billion, 200-acre proposal after residents raised water and energy concerns | [MultiState](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2025/10/2/data-centers-confront-local-opposition-across-america) |
| **Prince George's County, Maryland** | 180-day moratorium on new data centers after 20,000+ signature petition | [MultiState](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2025/10/2/data-centers-confront-local-opposition-across-america) |
| **Indigenous communities (Oklahoma, Southwest)** | Projects moving forward without public input under NDAs that block elected officials from sharing details with constituents | [Public Radio Tulsa](https://www.publicradiotulsa.org/local-regional/2025-10-07/indigenous-environmental-advocates-say-data-centers-risk-water-culture-and-informed-consent) |
| **Northeast Pennsylvania** | Residents fear negative impacts on water resources from proposed facilities | [WVIA News](https://www.wvia.org/news/local/2026-02-04/data-centers-residents-fear-negative-impacts-on-water-resources-as-data-centers-are-proposed-across-northeast-pa) |

### 7.3 The NDA Problem

A review of 31 Virginia municipalities with existing or proposed data centers found that **25 (80%) had non-disclosure agreements** with developers, limiting public access to information about project scale, resource needs, and potential impacts.

Source: [Brookings — "Local Implications of Data Centers for Rural Communities"](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/local-implications-data-centers-rural-communities-us/)

### 7.4 Why This Pattern Exists

Data centers follow the same siting logic as other heavy industry — cheap land, available power, and permissive local governance. These conditions correlate with lower-income, less politically connected communities. Historical industrial zoning policies have long resulted in the concentration of polluting industries in poor and minority communities. Data centers are the latest iteration of this pattern.

Source: [UAB Human Rights Blog](https://sites.uab.edu/humanrights/2025/10/02/construction-and-consequences-the-human-impacts-of-artificial-intelligence-data-centers/)

### 7.5 Community Resistance

Communities are pushing back, and at significant scale:
- **$64 billion** in data center projects blocked or delayed from May 2024 to March 2025
- An additional **$98 billion** blocked or delayed from March to June 2025
- **140+ activist groups across 24 states** organizing against new projects
- **230+ organizations** joined Food & Water Watch in calling for a federal moratorium on new data center construction (December 2025)
- The **NAACP** released a 2026 playbook for frontline communities and hosted a national summit (December 2025) on data center environmental justice

Sources:
- [Data Center Watch — $64B+ blocked/delayed](https://www.datacenterwatch.org/report)
- [NPR — Environmental groups call for moratorium](https://www.npr.org/2025/12/09/nx-s1-5637532/environmental-groups-call-for-a-moratorium-on-data-center-construction)
- [NAACP — Playbook for Frontline Communities](https://naacp.org/articles/naacp-releases-playbook-frontline-communities-stop-dirty-data-centers)
- [NAACP Data Center Summit](https://spokesman-recorder.com/2025/12/27/naacp-data-center-summit/)

### 7.6 Relevance to WaterLens

This is where the global vs local distinction matters most. When we say "AI is 0.01% of global freshwater," that's true but irrelevant to someone in Mansfield, Georgia whose well went dry. The tool should:
- Show the user's estimated water impact honestly (it's small)
- Note that data center water impact is highly localized
- Not dismiss legitimate community concerns about facility siting
- Acknowledge that the infrastructure powering AI has real local consequences even if the per-user impact is tiny

## 8. Provider Transparency: What Exists Today

### 8.1 The SDxCentral Hyperscaler Scorecard (July 2023)

SDxCentral published a sustainability scorecard rating Google, Microsoft, and AWS on publicly available data. This is the closest existing precedent to a provider transparency comparison. Scores were based on sustainability targets, emissions totals, included emissions categories, legacy emissions strategy, renewable energy, and data center PUE.

| Provider | Score | Key strengths | Key weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Google** | 4/5 | First to claim carbon neutral (2007); 24/7 CFE commitment; reports PUE (1.10); 120% water replenishment goal by 2030; addressed legacy emissions | Scope 3 emissions only partially reported |
| **Microsoft** | 3/5 | Carbon negative by 2030 goal; $1B climate fund; 100% renewable by 2025; water positive by 2030; 90% hardware reuse/recycle target | Does not report average or older data center PUE; 38% of energy still nonrenewable at time of report |
| **AWS/Amazon** | 2/5 | 100% renewable by 2025 (ahead of schedule); water positive by 2030; server lifespan extensions | Much larger total footprint; only market-based (not location-based) emissions reported; does not report PUE; has not addressed legacy emissions |

**Notable:** This scorecard focused on carbon/energy, with water mentioned only via pledges. No scorecard exists that rates providers specifically on water transparency.

Source: [SDxCentral — "Hyperscaler sustainability scorecard"](https://www.sdxcentral.com/analysis/hyperscaler-sustainability-scorecard-rating-googles-microsofts-and-aws-environmental-strategies/) (July 2023)

### 8.2 The Transparency Gap (AGU Advances, Privette et al. 2026)

A peer-reviewed commentary in *AGU Advances* provides the strongest academic case for water transparency:

**Key findings:**
- Data centers are "among the **least transparent industrial users of water**"
- **Fewer than 1/3 of operators** even track WUE (World Economic Forum, 2024)
- **97% of water** consumed comes from **municipal drinking water systems** — many at capacity
- Reported figures rarely distinguish **direct vs indirect** water use — a critical distinction for local planning
- Local governments and utilities sign **NDAs** that block public access, and water utilities **redact or block** usage data citing trade secrets
- **No standardized reporting** exists — different companies report different metrics in incomparable ways

**Specific examples from the paper:**
- Google's Council Bluffs, Iowa facility: **21% of total city water** (980M gallons in 2023)
- The Dalles, Oregon: **29% of city water demand** (355M gallons, tripled in 5 years)
- Mesa, Arizona: proposed facility would consume **2/3 of city's daily water supply**

**The paper's recommendations:**
1. Standardize facility-level water reporting
2. Integrate water availability assessment into site selection
3. Require disclosure in economic incentive agreements
4. Mandate water risk assessments for new facilities
5. Stronger collaboration between operators and local utilities

Source: Privette, A. P., Barros, A., & Cai, X. (2026). *AGU Advances*, 7, e2025AV002140. https://doi.org/10.1029/2025AV002140

### 8.3 Fast Company / The Conversation Investigation (August 2025)

Researchers in water law and policy at the Great Lakes analyzed public records, government documents, and sustainability reports from top data center companies. Key finding: "technology companies don't always reveal how much water their data centers use" and there is "significant variability in both how much water companies' data centers used and how much specific information their reports actually provided."

The investigation confirmed that corporate sustainability reports offered the most detail — but even those varied wildly in granularity. A forthcoming *Rutgers Computer and Technology Law Journal* article documents their full methodology.

Source: [Fast Company — "Tech companies rarely reveal how much water data centers use"](https://www.fastcompany.com/91388269/google-meta-water-use-data-centers-research) (August 2025)

### 8.4 What Doesn't Exist Yet

No public tool or scorecard currently:
- Rates AI providers specifically on **water** transparency (SDxCentral focused on carbon)
- Compares providers on **AI-specific** water or energy disclosure
- Shows users of ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Perplexity how transparent their provider is about water
- Rates providers on whether they publish **per-facility** vs **aggregate-only** data
- Tracks whether providers disclose water use for **AI workloads** separately from general data center use (none do)

**Existing frameworks:**
- **CDP** — scores 23,100+ companies on climate/water/forests, but general corporate disclosure, not AI-specific. Amazon and Meta had no public score in 2025.
- **Greenpeace Clicking Clean** — rated cloud providers on renewable energy (last major global edition: 2017, effectively dormant)
- **EU Data Center Reporting** — mandatory for facilities ≥500kW IT power, covering energy, water, capacity, waste heat. Next deadline May 2026 for calendar year 2025.
- **US Executive Order (Jan 2025)** — directed DOE to draft AI data center reporting requirements covering full lifecycle
